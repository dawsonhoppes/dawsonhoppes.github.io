    Divide

    Divide is a group pay application designed to simplify restaurant payment.

    When you log onto the app you are able to check into the restaurant you are at, join a table with friends, select what you ate, and divide your payment. Everyone on the app has linked their credit cards so that the app is able to transfer funds automatically.
    The Problem


    Group payment at restaurants is a complicated multi-step process that places unnecessary stress on us and our friends.
    Context

    This was an individual project completed for my senior thesis at Biola University.



    Challenges

    Divide was my first product design project. I studied Cinema in undergrad, but I knew I wanted to transition into UX so I decided to challenge myself and create Divide for my senior thesis.

    I had no previous experience with UX tools or design concepts so it was all a learning experience. I discovered I loved prototyping and creating user-flows for applications. This project led me to formally pursue UX and apply to my HCI masters program at the University of Maryland.

    Finding the Problem

    In order to create my project I knew I needed to find a problem to solve. I thought for a few weeks about how I could try to solve something related to my university, or something at my job, but I was having a hard time finding just the right thing to solve.

    I took a break from a brainstorming session to go out to dinner with a large group of friends. I was having such a good time with everyone, until the check came. We asked the waitress is she could divide our check and they weren't able to, so everyone in my party started pulling out their phone calculators to see how much they needed to pay. Then we all started to venmo one friend our portion of the dinner so they could pay the waitress.

    And then it hit me, this is the problem I should solve! There had to be a simpler way to do this and to simplify the group payment experience.





    User Interviews

    Before I started designing I knew I should interview a few people to see if I was the only one experiencing this frustration. Additionally, I wanted to find out if there were any other pain-points I didn't see.

    I went out and interviewed 5 other people from all different ages. Here are some of the most poignant quotes and feelings I captured through my interviews.

    "I hate being the person that pays. Everyone forgets to venmo me, or they won't send me the appropriate amount."
    "I never know how to split tip and tax when I'm with a group."
    "I feel bad for the waitress when I'm with a big group, if they are nice they end up splitting the check for us but it sucks she has to complete 5 different transactions just to finish up one of her tables."
    "It's odd we have so many apps for the restaurant experience. We have Yelp to find places and Venmo to pay each other, but what if there was one for all?"

    Project Focus

    Based off my user interviews, I identified these potential problems with our current group payment systems.

    • One person gets stuck with the check and sometimes pays more than they should.
    • Sometimes we aren't sure how to divide the check.
    • Group payment is also a hassle for the restaurant staff.
    • There are too many apps for the restaurant experience.




    Design



    Concept

    After refining the project focus and problems, I was able to start brainstorming and creating solutions to those problems. Throughout my ideation phase I kept coming back to this idea of a one-stop app for all things food, that could suggest restaurants and provide a simple process for group payment at those restaurants.


    Sketching

    After identifying the problems I started to sketch out some possible solutions. It is always helpful to start drawing out your ideas and getting your big picture concepts down on paper.

    I was able to take these ideas to my faculty advisor and talk through some of the app's functionality before I continued in my design process.

    Prototyping

    After I knew the pages I needed to make I was able to start designing mock-ups and a hi-fidelity prototype. Since I had no experience coding at this point, I decided to create my mockup in Adobe Xd because I was familiar with the Adobe CC suite from my graphic design background.




    Usability Testing

    After I had made the hi-fidelity mockup in Xd I was able to take the prototype to a few users and watch them interact with the program. I took notes of the things that worked, the things that didn't, and the things I didn't expect them to do. I was then able to go back in an refine my designs before the final presentation.
    FINAL PRODUCT
    Sign Up


    It is really easy to get started with Divide. Just download the app, tap sign up and answer a few questions. All we need is your contact info, a picture, and your credit card number. Then you are ready to go!








    Check-In

    The app uses your location to pull up restaurants near you. After you select the restaurant you are at, you are able to check in to your table and connect with your friends.


    Pay

    After you eat, the app will ask you what food you ate and how much you want to tip. Then either pay for the meal with your card, or have one of your friends pay. After someone pays, the app will divide everyone's portion of the bill and transfer the appropriate funds. It is easy!








    Review

    After the app has transferred the payments there is an optional review form. You can leave a review of the restaurant and even add some pictures!


    Functionality

    Explore the videos below to see the app in action. There are six main parts to the app: log-in and sign up, the main menu, divide (group pay), restaurant discovery, reviews, and a coming soon section.





    Home

    The main menu is simple and clean with minimal clutter. The large yellow divide symbol is how they access the group pay functionality.



    Divide

    This group pay function makes it easy to go out with friends. Restaurants link their systems with the app to provide necessary information like a list of items ordered at the table.



    Discover

    Discover is similar to Yelp. Users are able to find restaurants based off their location and even make a reservation through the app!



    Login / Sign up

    Since Divide transfers money, the app needs to collect necessary information like credit card numbers and save them for future visits.



    Reviews

    Users can do a little research before they go to a restaurant. They can also write a review for the restaurants they have eaten at.



    Coming Soon

    This feature allows the user to look at the restaurants coming soon to their area. They can even adjust the location to explore nearby spots.




    Conclusion


    As previously mentioned, Divide was my first product design project. While I learned a lot about different tools and systems for creating prototypes, the most important thing I learned was just how important it is to bring your user into the design process.

    If I hadn't talked to other people in the beginning of the project I would have missed important pain points that would later turned into design insights. This helped me avoid designing for myself, or from the "I" perspective.

    If I were to do this project over again I would spend more time diving into user research. In my masters courses I am studying UX research methods and learning how to create solutions through contextual design. While I am pleased with the result of this project, I can see that my designs would have been even stronger if I had more time to conduct contextual inquiries and make additional models from that data. It would also be nice to talk to employees of restaurants to get a deeper understanding of their perspective on this problem.

    In this process I also realized just how valuable it is to conduct usability studies throughout the design process. Users do unexpected things and can sometimes interact with our designs in ways we never thought. Going through the user studies showed me just how important it is to have posture of humility in the design phase, so you can admit when something is wrong and make necessary changes. We are designing for the users experience not our own.
